What is a Fractal?

    "...beautiful, damn hard, increasingly useful. That's fractals."
    -Benoit Mandelbrot

Definition
  • A fractal is an infinitely complex pattern that is self-similar across many different scales.
  • A fractal is created by recursion through a simple process in an ongoing feedback loop.

History
    Gaston Julia (1893 - 1978)
    • French mathematician.
    • Published a book on the iteration of rational functions in 1918.
    • His work was some of the first on fractal sets.
    • Devised the formula for the Julia set, which led Mandelbrot to discover the Mandelbrot set.

    Waclaw Sierpinski (1882 - 1969)
    • Polish mathematician.
    • Mostly known for his contributions to set theory and number theory.
    • His work in set theory set the way for these "fractal sets" to be drawn out.
    • Well known for the Sierpinski triangle, but devised many other Sierpinski-style fractals.

    Helge von Koch (1870 - 1924)
    • Swedish mathematician
    • Published a paper on a continuous curve without tangents constructible from elementary geometry in 1904
    • Devised the formula for the Koch curve (also known as Koch snowflake).
    • One of the earliest pioneers of fractal sets

    Benoit Mandelbrot (1924 - 2010)
    • Emeritus professor at Yale University.
    • Explored Julia's iterated functions, and found a simpler equation that included all the Julia sets.
    • Devised the formula for the Mandelbrot set.
    • Coined the term "fractal".
Uses and Why There is Interest
  • Simple solution to capture the enormous detail and irregularity of clouds and landscapes.
  • Efficient way to draw realistic natural objects on a computer screen.
  • Used to model ecosystems.
  • Used in today's cell phone antennas to maximize receptive power in a minimum amount of space.


  • Used in image compression software (see image below).

Chaos Theory and Relationship to Fractals
    "Chaos: When the present determines the future, but the approximate present does not approximately determine the future."
    - Edward Lorenz
  • Study of the behavior of dynamical systems that are highly sensitive to initial conditions.
  • Small differences in initial conditions yield widely diverging outcomes.
  • Even though the systems are deterministic, long-time prediction can be impossible.
  • Not random!
    Example
    • In this example, we have a predictable water wheel with controlled amounts of water dripped into buckets. But as the flow increases, the system becomes increasingly less linear.

Recursion
    Recursion: A computer programming technique whereby a function is defined so as to invoke itself.



    Feedback loop: A chain of cause-and-effect events that forms a loop, the event is said to "feed back" into itself.
